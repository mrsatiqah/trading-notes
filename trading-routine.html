Trading Habits

Building a Trading Routine That Sticks

Turning everything in this series into a sustainable daily and weekly trading routine, the final piece that ties thirty articles of knowledge into consistent action.

This is the final article in this series. We've covered many important topics, from reading candlestick charts to managing risk and controlling emotions. Now it's time to put everything together into one simple routine you can follow every day.

Knowing a lot about trading is helpful. But knowledge alone doesn't make you a better trader. Without a routine, it's easy to forget what you've learned or make emotional decisions.

The traders who stay profitable for many years are not always the smartest. Most of the time, they're simply the ones who can follow the same good habits every day.

Discipline Is Better Than Motivation

Motivation feels great, but it doesn't stay forever. Some days you'll feel excited to trade, and some days you won't. That's why a routine is so important. A good trading routine doesn't depend on how you feel. You just follow the process, one day at a time.

A Simple Daily Trading Routine

This is only an example. Feel free to adjust it based on your timezone, the market you trade, and your daily schedule.

Before the Market Opens
Prepare Yourself
  • Check today's economic calendar
  • Read any important overnight news
  • Look for market gaps or major events
  • Take a moment to clear your mind and focus on following your plan instead of chasing profits
When the Market Opens
Observe First
  • Watch the market for the first 15 to 30 minutes
  • Don't rush into a trade
  • Mark important support and resistance levels
  • Pay attention to the overall market direction and volatility
During the Trading Session
Execute Your Plan
  • Only take trades that match your trading rules
  • Use the correct position size based on your risk management
  • Avoid staring at the charts every second while waiting for setups
  • If there's no valid setup, don't force a trade
After the Market Closes
Review Your Day
  • Record every trade in your trading journal
  • Write down one thing you did well and one thing you can improve
  • Once you're done, close your charts and enjoy the rest of your day

Your Routine Should Be Weekly Too

A good routine isn't only about what you do every day. You also need time every week to review your progress and give yourself proper rest.

Mon
Trade and prepare your plan for the week
Tue
Trade as usual
Wed
Trade and do a quick journal review
Thu
Trade as usual
Fri
Trade and complete your weekly review
Sat
Rest. Stay away from the charts.
Sun
Light reading or study, then prepare for the coming week
Rest isn't something you earn after working hard. Rest is part of becoming a better trader.

Four Things Every Good Trading Routine Needs

Keep It Simple
A simple routine that you can follow every day is much better than a perfect routine that only lasts two weeks.
Set Clear Boundaries
Have a fixed time to start and stop trading. This helps you avoid overtrading and also prevents burnout.
Review Your Performance
Spending a few minutes reviewing your trades every day and every week helps you learn much faster than simply placing more trades.
Don't Forget to Recover
Everyone needs breaks. Taking time away from the market helps you stay fresh and think more clearly when you return.

What Should You Do Next?

Don't think of this series as something you read once and forget. Come back whenever you need a refresher. Even experienced traders revisit the basics because the fundamentals never stop being important.

As you gain more experience, you'll notice these lessons start making even more sense. Keep learning. Keep reviewing. Most importantly, keep following your routine. Small improvements made consistently over time often lead to the biggest results.

Key Takeaways

  • Discipline is more reliable than motivation.
  • Build your day around preparation, observation, execution, and review.
  • Have a weekly routine that includes both review sessions and rest days.
  • A strong routine is built on four things: simplicity, boundaries, reflection, and recovery.
  • Knowledge only becomes skill when you practise it consistently.
  • Keep returning to the fundamentals as your trading experience grows.
