Common Trading Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The recurring errors that trip up most beginners, and practical ways to sidestep them.

If you've read this far into the series, you already know the theory: manage your risk, size your positions properly, understand your own psychology. So why do so many traders, including smart, disciplined people in every other area of their lives, still blow up their accounts?

Because knowing a mistake and catching yourself in the act of making it are two completely different skills. Almost every trader who has ever lost money can tell you exactly what they did wrong, after the fact. The trick is recognizing the pattern while it's still happening, not three days later while reviewing the account statement.

The Same Mistakes, Different Traders

Almost nobody blows up their account with a completely original mistake. It's nearly always one of a small handful of well-known traps, the same ones that have been catching traders since markets existed.

The Mistakes That Show Up Again and Again

These aren't ranked by severity, they tend to arrive in combinations, one mistake often triggering the next.

Trading Without a Plan
Entering a trade because "it looks good" rather than because it meets specific, written criteria. Without a plan, every decision becomes a fresh negotiation with your emotions.
Skipping the Stop Loss
Telling yourself "I'll watch it closely instead." You won't. The moment it matters most is exactly when you'll be away from the screen or too emotional to act.
Overtrading
Taking five mediocre setups because the good ones aren't showing up today. Boredom is not a trading signal, but it feels like one.
Risking Too Much Per Trade
Sizing positions based on conviction ("I'm really sure about this one") instead of a fixed percentage rule. Confidence and correctness are not the same thing.
Copying Trades Blindly
Taking a signal from a Telegram group or influencer without understanding the setup, the risk, or the exit. You inherit the loss but never learn the lesson.
Not Keeping a Journal
Trading a hundred times and remembering none of it clearly. Without records, every mistake gets a chance to repeat itself, disguised as a new situation.
Strategy Hopping
Abandoning a strategy after three losing trades to chase the next one that looks more promising. Most strategies need a much larger sample size to prove themselves.
Trading the News Blindly
Jumping into a trade the second a headline drops, without waiting to see how the market actually reacts. The first move after big news is often the wrong one.

Bad Habits vs Good Habits

Most of these mistakes have a direct, learnable opposite. Here's the side-by-side:

The Habit That Hurts
  • Entering trades on impulse or "gut feeling"
  • Widening stop losses when a trade goes wrong
  • Increasing size to "make back" a loss quickly
  • Checking charts constantly out of anxiety
  • Comparing your results to someone else's highlight reel
The Habit That Helps
  • Entering only when your written criteria are met
  • Respecting the original stop loss, no exceptions
  • Keeping size consistent regardless of recent results
  • Checking charts at scheduled times only
  • Comparing this month's you to last month's you

The Mistake Nobody Talks About: Being Right for the Wrong Reason

This one is sneaky because it doesn't look like a mistake at all, it looks like a win. You break your own rules, ignore your stop, or size up impulsively, and the trade works out anyway. Nothing reinforces bad behavior faster than a lucky profit.

The Danger of a Lucky Win

A profitable trade taken the wrong way teaches you exactly the wrong lesson: that the shortcut works. It doesn't, not consistently, and the market eventually collects on every shortcut you've taken.

This is why professional traders grade themselves on process, not outcome. A well-planned trade that loses is still a good trade. A rule-breaking trade that wins is still a mistake, just one that hasn't been punished yet.

If You've Already Made Several of These

Almost every trader reading this list will recognize themselves in at least three or four of these mistakes. That's normal, not a reason for shame.

The Reframe

Recognizing a mistake after the fact is the first skill. Catching it mid-trade is the second. Preventing it before you even open the platform is the third. You don't need to skip straight to mastery, just keep moving one step closer.

Your Fix-It Plan

Three Things to Do This Week

  • Write down your entry criteria for your next 5 trades before you place them, not after
  • Pick one mistake from the list above that you recognize most in yourself, and track only that one for two weeks
  • Set a fixed stop loss on every trade before entry, and treat moving it as a broken rule, not a judgment call

Key Takeaways

  • Nearly every account-ending mistake is one of a small, well-known set of traps
  • Trading without a plan turns every decision into an emotional negotiation
  • Skipping stop losses fails you at exactly the moment you need protection most
  • A profitable trade taken the wrong way is still a mistake, it just hasn't been punished yet
  • Judge yourself on process and rule-following, not on the outcome of any single trade
  • Recognizing a mistake after the fact is progress, even before you can catch it in the moment
