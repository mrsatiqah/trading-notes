Risk-Reward Ratios: The Math Behind Profits

You can be wrong more often than you're right and still get rich. You can also be right most of the time and still go broke. The difference is one simple ratio.

Ask a new trader what makes a good trader, and they'll almost always say the same thing: "being right more often than you're wrong." It sounds obvious. It's also completely wrong.

A trader who wins only 30% of the time can be wildly profitable. A trader who wins 70% of the time can slowly bleed their account to zero. The missing piece isn't win rate. It's risk-reward ratio, the relationship between how much you stand to lose versus how much you stand to gain on every single trade.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Win rate feels good. It's the number people brag about. But risk-reward ratio is the number that actually determines whether your account grows or shrinks over hundreds of trades.

What Is a Risk-Reward Ratio?

A risk-reward ratio simply compares how much you're risking on a trade to how much you're aiming to gain. If you risk $50 to potentially make $150, your risk-reward ratio is 1:3, you're risking one dollar for every three you could win.

The Basic Formula
Risk-Reward Ratio = Potential Loss ÷ Potential Gain
Example: You enter at $50, set a stop loss at $48 (risking $2), and a target at $56 (potential gain of $6). Risk-Reward = $2 : $6 = 1:3.

The Breakeven Math Every Trader Needs

Here's the calculation that changes how you see every trade: your risk-reward ratio determines the minimum win rate you need just to break even.

Breakeven Win Rate Formula
Breakeven Win Rate = Risk ÷ (Risk + Reward)
Example: With a 1:2 risk-reward ratio, Breakeven Win Rate = 1 ÷ (1 + 2) = 33%. You only need to be right one out of three times to break even.

Why This Matters

This is why professional traders obsess over their risk-reward ratio before they even look at a chart pattern. A favorable ratio buys you room to be wrong, a lot, and still come out ahead.

Expectancy: The One Number That Matters

Win rate and risk-reward ratio only tell half the story each. Combine them, and you get expectancy, the true measure of whether your trading approach makes money over the long run.

Expectancy Formula
Expectancy = (Win Rate × Average Win) − (Loss Rate × Average Loss)
Example: Win rate 40%, average win $300, loss rate 60%, average loss $100.
Expectancy = (0.40 × $300) − (0.60 × $100) = $120 − $60 = +$60 per trade.

A positive expectancy means that, over a large enough number of trades, your account grows, even though the majority of individual trades might be losers. A negative expectancy means you're guaranteed to lose money over time, no matter how exciting the wins feel in the moment.

Thinking in R-Multiples

Professional traders rarely think in dollars while a trade is open. Instead, they think in "R", where 1R equals the amount risked on the trade. A trade that makes twice what you risked is a "+2R" trade. A full stop-out loss is "-1R."

Why R-Multiples Are Powerful

Thinking in R removes the emotional weight of dollar figures and lets you evaluate your trading system objectively. "I average +0.4R per trade" tells you far more about your edge than "I made $2,340 last month," because it scales with account size and stays consistent no matter how big your account grows.

Two Traders, Same Setup, Very Different Outcomes

Let's compare two traders using the exact same strategy and win rate, but different risk-reward discipline:

Trader A: No Risk-Reward Discipline

Wins 50% of trades but cuts winners short out of fear and lets losers run out of hope.

Average win: $50  |  Average loss: $150

Expectancy: (0.50 × $50) − (0.50 × $150) = −$50 per trade

Despite winning half the time, this trader is mathematically guaranteed to lose money over time.

Trader B: Strict 1:3 Discipline

Wins only 35% of trades, but every trade risks $50 to make $150, no exceptions.

Average win: $150  |  Average loss: $50

Expectancy: (0.35 × $150) − (0.65 × $50) = +$20 per trade

Wrong nearly two-thirds of the time, yet steadily profitable over hundreds of trades.

The Real Lesson

Trader A "feels" more successful day to day because they win more often. Trader B "feels" like they're losing constantly. But only one of them is actually building wealth. This is exactly why so many traders quit right before their edge would have paid off, they're judging themselves by the wrong number.

Mistakes That Wreck the Math

Cutting Winners Short
Taking profit early "just to lock something in" turns a planned 1:3 trade into an accidental 1:0.5, destroying the very math that made the trade worth taking.
Moving the Stop Loss
Widening a stop mid-trade to "give it room" quietly changes your risk-reward ratio for the worse, often without you even noticing until the account statement does.
Chasing Unrealistic Targets
Setting a 1:10 target that the market rarely reaches looks great on paper but leads to constantly missed exits and frustration.
Ignoring Costs
Spreads, commissions, and slippage quietly erode your reward side of the ratio. A "1:2" trade on paper can behave like 1:1.7 once real costs are factored in.

Key Takeaways

  • Win rate alone means nothing without knowing your risk-reward ratio
  • Breakeven win rate = Risk ÷ (Risk + Reward), a 1:2 ratio only needs a 33% win rate
  • Expectancy combines win rate and risk-reward into the single number that predicts long-term results
  • Thinking in R-multiples removes emotional attachment to dollar amounts
  • A trader who wins less often can still out-earn a trader who wins more often, if the math favors them
  • Cutting winners short and moving stop losses are the two fastest ways to destroy good risk-reward math
  • Always account for spreads, commissions, and slippage when calculating your real reward
