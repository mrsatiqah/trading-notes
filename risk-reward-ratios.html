Risk-Reward Ratio: The Math Behind Profitable Trading

You can lose more trades than you win and still make money. You can also win most of your trades and still lose your entire account. The difference is one simple concept.

Ask most beginner traders "what makes someone a good trader?" and many will answer: "winning more trades than losing trades." It sounds logical, but in trading, this is not always true.

A trader who wins only 30% of their trades can still become profitable. A trader who wins 70% of their trades can still lose money. The missing piece is not just your win rate, it's how much you make when you are right compared to how much you lose when you are wrong. That is called risk-reward ratio.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Win rate feels good, people like to say "I have an 80% winning rate." But win rate alone does not tell the full story, a trader can have many winning trades but one big loss can erase all previous profits. Professional traders focus more on the relationship between risk and reward, because over hundreds of trades, the math is what decides whether an account grows or shrinks.

What Is Risk-Reward Ratio?

Risk-reward ratio compares how much you are willing to lose versus how much you expect to make. If you risk $50 to make $150, your risk-reward ratio is 1:3, meaning for every $1 you risk, you are trying to make $3.

The Basic Formula
Risk-Reward Ratio = Potential Loss : Potential Gain
Example: Entry price $50, stop loss $48 (risk $2), target price $56 (potential reward $6). Risk-Reward = $2 : $6 = 1:3.

The Break-Even Math Every Trader Should Know

Your risk-reward ratio tells you how many winning trades you need just to avoid losing money.

Break-Even Formula
Break-Even Win Rate = Risk ÷ (Risk + Reward)
Example: With a 1:2 risk-reward ratio, 1 ÷ (1 + 2) = 33%. You only need to win about 33% of your trades to break even, you can lose 2 out of 3 trades and still survive.

Why Risk-Reward Matters

This is why experienced traders think about risk-reward before entering a trade. A good risk-reward ratio gives you room to be wrong, because the reality is you will never be right all the time, even professional traders lose. The difference is they control how much they lose and how much they make.

Expectancy: The Number That Really Matters

Win rate and risk-reward ratio are important, but when combined together, they create something more powerful: expectancy. Expectancy tells you whether your trading system has a positive or negative result over many trades.

Expectancy Formula
Expectancy = (Win Rate × Average Win) − (Loss Rate × Average Loss)
Example: Win rate 40%, average winning trade $300, loss rate 60%, average losing trade $100.
(0.40 × $300) − (0.60 × $100) = $120 − $60 = +$60 per trade. Statistically, every trade has a positive expectation.

Positive expectancy means your strategy makes money over a large number of trades. Negative expectancy means your strategy loses money over time, even if you have some big winning trades. A few lucky wins do not mean a strategy is profitable, the math must work consistently.

Thinking in R-Multiples

Professional traders usually do not think about money while inside a trade. Instead, they think in terms of R. 1R means the amount you risk on a trade, for example if you risk $100, your 1R = $100. If you lose, that's -1R = -$100, if you make double your risk, that's +2R = +$200.

Why R-Multiples Are Useful

Thinking in R removes emotional attachment to money. Instead of saying "I lost $500 today," you think "I lost -1R." This makes your trading easier to analyze, your account size may change, but your trading performance can still be measured consistently.

Two Traders, Same Strategy, Different Results

Let's compare two traders. Both use the same strategy and have the same number of winning trades, but their risk management is different.

Trader A: No Risk-Reward Discipline

Takes profit too early and holds losing trades too long.

Win rate: 50%  |  Average win: $50  |  Average loss: $150

Expectancy: (0.50 × $50) − (0.50 × $150) = $25 − $75 = −$50 per trade

Even with a 50% win rate, this trader loses money.

Trader B: Follows 1:3 Risk-Reward

Accepts small losses and lets winners reach the target.

Win rate: 35%  |  Average win: $150  |  Average loss: $50

Expectancy: (0.35 × $150) − (0.65 × $50) = $52.50 − $32.50 = +$20 per trade

This trader loses more often but still makes money.

The Real Lesson

Trader A feels better because they win more frequently. Trader B feels uncomfortable because they lose many trades. But only one trader has positive mathematics. Many traders quit because they judge themselves based on short-term results instead of understanding expectancy.

Mistakes That Destroy Risk-Reward

Taking Profit Too Early
A trader plans a 1:3 trade, but when price moves slightly, "I better take profit before it reverses." Suddenly a planned 1:3 trade becomes 1:0.5, the whole advantage disappears.
Moving Stop Loss
"I will give this trade more space." The problem is your original risk changes, a small planned loss can become a much bigger loss.
Setting Unrealistic Targets
A 1:10 target looks attractive, but if the market rarely reaches it, you may constantly miss exits. A realistic target is better than a perfect target that rarely happens.
Ignoring Trading Costs
Real trading has spread, commission, and slippage. A trade that looks like 1:2 on paper may become smaller after costs, always calculate the real numbers.

Final Thoughts

Trading is not about being right all the time.

It is about making sure your wins are bigger than your losses over the long run.

Key Takeaways

  • Win rate alone does not determine trading success
  • Risk-reward ratio shows how much you risk compared to your potential gain
  • A 1:2 risk-reward ratio only needs around 33% win rate to break even
  • Expectancy combines win rate and risk-reward to measure long-term profitability
  • Thinking in R-multiples helps remove emotional decisions
  • A trader with fewer winning trades can still outperform a trader with a higher win rate
  • Cutting winners too early destroys good risk-reward mathematics
  • Moving stop losses can turn a good trade into a bad one
  • Always include real trading costs when calculating your results
