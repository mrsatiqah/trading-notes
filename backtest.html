Backtesting Your Strategy Before Going Live

Test your edge against history before you ever risk a single dollar on it. This is the step most beginners skip, and the one professionals never do.

Before you risk real money, you should know if your strategy actually works.

Many beginners skip this step. They find a strategy on YouTube, read about it in a book, or hear someone talking about it online. Then they immediately start trading with real money.

The problem is, they have no idea whether the strategy has an edge or not. That's where backtesting comes in.

Think of It Like a Flight Simulator

Imagine getting on a plane with a pilot who has never practiced flying, no training, no simulator, no emergency drills. Most people wouldn't feel comfortable. Trading is no different. Backtesting is like a flight simulator for traders, it lets you practise your strategy using historical market data before putting real money at risk. If your strategy fails, you lose nothing except time, and that's much better than losing your trading account.

Why Backtesting Is So Important

Backtesting simply means applying your trading rules to past market data to see how the strategy would have performed. It helps answer one important question: does this strategy actually have an edge, or does it just sound good? Instead of guessing, you'll have data to support your decision.

What You'll Learn From a Backtest

A proper backtest gives you much more than just your win rate. It also shows how often the strategy wins, the average size of your winning and losing trades, the biggest drawdown you might experience, and how the strategy performs in different market conditions, whether trending, moving sideways, or highly volatile. This gives you a much more realistic expectation before trading live.

How to Backtest a Strategy

1
Write Down Your Rules
Be as specific as possible. Your rules should clearly explain when to enter, where to place your stop loss, when to take profit, and how much to risk on each trade. If your rules are vague, your backtest won't be reliable.
2
Collect Historical Data
Use enough chart history to cover different market conditions. A good starting point is two to five years of historical data or at least 100 trade setups. The more data you test, the more confidence you'll have in the results.
3
Apply Your Rules
Go through the charts one trade at a time. Follow your rules exactly as written. Don't change the rules halfway through just because a setup looks better in hindsight. The goal is to be consistent.
4
Record Every Trade
Keep a record of every trade, whether it's a winner or a loser. A spreadsheet works perfectly for this. The more organised your records are, the easier it is to analyse the strategy later.
5
Review the Results
After you've finished testing, calculate the important numbers. These statistics will tell you whether the strategy is worth considering.

Important Numbers to Track

Win Rate
The percentage of trades that ended in profit
Average R Multiple
The average amount you make or lose compared to the amount you risk
Expectancy
How much you can expect to make or lose per trade over a large number of trades
Maximum Drawdown
The biggest losing period the strategy experienced, so you can prepare mentally before trading live
Sample Size
The more trades you test, the more reliable your results become
Consistency
Whether the strategy performs reasonably well across different market conditions instead of only during one specific period
Don't Be Fooled by Small Samples

Winning ten trades in a row feels great, but it doesn't necessarily prove your strategy works, sometimes it's simply good luck. Try to backtest at least 50 to 100 trades, or even more if possible. A larger sample gives you much more confidence in your results.

Different Ways to Backtest

Manual Chart Review
Easiest for Beginners
Scroll through historical charts and manually mark every trade based on your rules. It takes time, but you'll become much better at recognising chart patterns.
Spreadsheet Tracking
Organise and Calculate
After identifying your trades, record everything in a spreadsheet. This makes it easy to calculate your statistics and compare results.
Backtesting Software
Fast but Technical
Many trading platforms include built-in backtesting tools. TradingView's Strategy Tester, for example, allows you to test coded strategies over several years of historical data within seconds, though it usually requires some programming knowledge.
Backtesting Isn't the Final Step

A strategy that performs well in backtesting still needs another test, called forward testing or paper trading. Trade the strategy in real market conditions without risking real money first. Markets are always changing, so it's important to see how your strategy performs today, not only in the past. Once you're confident with the results, you can slowly move to live trading using small position sizes.

Common Backtesting Mistakes

Looking Into the Future
Don't let hindsight influence your decisions. Only use the information that would have been available at that point in time.
Curve Fitting
Some traders keep adjusting their strategy until it looks perfect on historical data. These "perfect" strategies often perform badly in live markets. Simple and consistent usually works better.
Ignoring Trading Costs
Remember to include commissions, spreads, and slippage. These costs can turn a profitable backtest into an unprofitable live strategy.
Testing Too Few Trades
Don't make conclusions after only 10 or 15 trades. A small sample doesn't tell you much. Always aim for at least 50 to 100 trades before deciding whether a strategy has an edge.

Final Thoughts

Backtesting takes time, and it can feel repetitive. But it's one of the best investments you can make as a trader.

Instead of risking money on a strategy you hope will work, you'll have real data to support your decisions.

Professional traders don't rely on luck. They rely on testing, statistics, and consistency. The more confidence you have in your strategy before going live, the easier it becomes to stick with it when the market gets challenging.

Key Takeaways

  • Backtesting lets you test a strategy using historical market data before risking real money
  • Write clear and specific trading rules before you begin
  • Track important statistics like win rate, expectancy, average R multiple, and maximum drawdown
  • Test at least 50 to 100 historical trades for more reliable results
  • You can backtest manually, with spreadsheets, or using specialised software
  • Follow a successful backtest with forward testing before trading live
  • Avoid hindsight bias, curve fitting, and ignoring trading costs
