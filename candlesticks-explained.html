How to Read Price Charts: Candlesticks Explained

Master the art of reading candlestick charts and decode the stories they tell about market battles between buyers and sellers.

Staring at your first trading chart can feel like trying to read hieroglyphics. Those colorful bars going up and down might look like abstract art, but they're actually telling you a detailed story about what happened between buyers and sellers during each time period.

Today we're going to decode the most common type of price chart: candlestick charts. By the end of this article, you'll be reading them like a trader who's been doing this for years.

The Market's Diary

Think of candlesticks as the market's diary. Each one captures a complete story of battle between bulls (buyers) and bears (sellers) during a specific time period, whether that's one minute, one hour, or one day.

Anatomy of a Candlestick

Before we get into patterns and strategies, let's understand what each candlestick is actually showing you. Every candlestick contains four crucial pieces of information and has specific visual components that tell the complete story.

Visual Components of a Candlestick
↑
Bullish Candle
Close higher than open (Green/White)
↓
Bearish Candle
Close lower than open (Red/Black)
Color Coding Note

Green (or white) candles: Close price is higher than open price (bullish)
Red (or black) candles: Close price is lower than open price (bearish)

The Four Key Prices

Every candlestick contains exactly four pieces of price information that together tell the complete story of that time period:

Open
The first price traded during that time period. Where the battle begins.
High
The highest price reached during that time period. Bulls' maximum advance.
Low
The lowest price reached during that time period. Bears' maximum advance.
Close
The last price traded during that time period. Who won the battle.
Real Example Story

Let's say you're looking at a daily chart of Apple stock, and you see a green candlestick:

The Setup: Apple opened the day at $150.

The Battle: During the day, bears pushed it down as low as $148, but bulls fought back and pushed it as high as $155.

The Resolution: By market close, bulls had won, closing at $153, creating a green candle.

Types of Candlestick Bodies

The size and color of the candlestick body tells you how intense the battle was and who had more control:

Large Bodies
Strong Conviction
Green large body: Strong buying pressure. Bulls dominated convincingly.
Red large body: Strong selling pressure. Bears were in complete control.
Large bodies suggest strong conviction in the direction of the move.
Small Bodies
Indecision
When the open and close are very close to each other, you get a small body or even just a line.
Indecision. Neither side could gain meaningful control. Often signals potential direction changes.
Perfect Doji
Perfect Balance
When open and close prices are exactly the same, you get a cross-like formation with just wicks.
Perfect balance between buyers and sellers, often precedes significant moves.

Understanding the Wicks

The wicks (shadows) are just as important as the bodies, they show what happened during the battle, not just who won.

Long Upper Wicks
Rejection at Highs
Green candle: Bulls pushed higher but couldn't hold. Red candle: Bears tried to rally but got overwhelmed.
Shows selling pressure at higher levels. Often indicates resistance.
Long Lower Wicks
Support at Lows
Green candle: Bears pushed lower but bulls fought back strongly. Red candle: Bears pushed down but couldn't sustain it.
Shows buying support at lower levels. Often indicates support.
No Wicks
Complete Dominance
Market opened near the low and closed near the high (or vice versa for red).
Very strong directional pressure with no significant counter-moves.

Common Single Candlestick Patterns

Now that you understand the basics, let's look at some significant single-candle formations:

Hammer
Bullish Reversal
Small body at the top, long lower wick (at least twice the body size), little to no upper wick.
Story: Bears pushed lower, but bulls fought back to close near session high. Potential reversal after a downtrend.
Shooting Star
Bearish Reversal
Small body at the bottom, long upper wick, little to no lower wick.
Story: Bulls pushed higher, but bears closed near session low. Potential reversal after an uptrend.
Spinning Top
Indecision
Very small body with upper and lower wicks of similar length.
Story: Both sides fought hard, neither gained control. Often appears before significant moves.
Marubozu
Strong Direction
Large body with no wicks (or very tiny ones).
Story: One side dominated completely. Very strong directional conviction.

Timeframes: The Same Story, Different Scales

The same stock can look completely different depending on which timeframe you're viewing. The timeframe you choose depends on your trading style and how long you plan to hold positions.

1-Minute Charts
Great for day traders who want to see every move, but can be noisy for beginners.
5-Minute Charts
Still detailed but slightly smoother than 1-minute charts.
1-Hour Charts
Good middle ground for day traders and swing traders.
Daily Charts
Most popular for swing traders and position traders. Less noise, clearer trends.
Weekly Charts
Used for long-term analysis and identifying major trends.
Key Insight

A stock might show a strong green daily candle while simultaneously showing red hourly candles. This is completely normal, different battles happen at different time scales.

Context is Everything

Individual candlesticks become powerful when you consider them in context. The same hammer pattern means different things in different situations:

After Strong Downtrend
Potential Reversal
A hammer after several red candles suggests potential buying interest and possible reversal.
High Significance: Buyers stepping in after sustained selling.
After Strong Uptrend
Normal Pullback
A hammer during an uptrend might just be a normal pullback before continuation higher.
Lower Significance: Just normal profit-taking in a healthy uptrend.
In Sideways Market
Minimal Significance
A hammer in a ranging market might not have much significance at all.
Minimal Significance: Just normal price action within the range.
Trading Wisdom

Successful traders don't just look at individual candles, they consider the overall trend, recent price action, and market context.

Practical Tips & Common Mistakes

Here are practical tips for improving your chart reading skills, plus common mistakes to avoid:

Start with Daily Charts
Daily charts are less noisy and easier to interpret. Master these before exploring shorter timeframes.
Look for Obvious Patterns First
Focus on the really obvious ones, clear hammers, shooting stars, large marubozu candles.
Consider Volume
A hammer with high volume is more significant than one with low volume.
Practice with Historical Charts
Go back in time on charts and practice identifying patterns and outcomes.
Don't See Patterns Everywhere
Not every candlestick is significant. Avoid forcing patterns where none exist.
Don't Ignore the Trend
A bearish pattern in a strong uptrend is less significant than the same pattern after a decline.
Wrong Timeframe Usage
Match your timeframe to your trading style, day trading vs swing trading need different charts.
Trading Single Candles Alone
Candlesticks work best when combined with support/resistance levels and other factors.

The Psychology Behind the Patterns

When you start thinking of candlesticks as representations of market psychology rather than just price movements, you'll begin to anticipate what might happen next.

Large Green Candles
Greed and FOMO driving buying.
Large Red Candles
Fear and panic driving selling.
Doji Patterns
Uncertainty and indecision.
Long Wicks
Rejection of certain price levels.
Building Your Skills

Daily Practice: Spend 10-15 minutes each day looking at charts.
Pattern Recognition: Keep a trading journal with screenshots of patterns.
Multiple Timeframes: Practice looking at the same stock on different timeframes.

Key Takeaways

  • Every candlestick contains four key prices: open, high, low, and close
  • Large bodies indicate strong conviction, small bodies suggest indecision
  • Long wicks show rejection at certain price levels and potential support/resistance
  • Context matters more than individual patterns - consider the overall trend
  • Different timeframes tell different stories about the same asset
  • Start with daily charts and obvious patterns before exploring complex formations
  • Practice with historical data to build pattern recognition skills
  • Don't trade on candlestick patterns alone - use them as part of a complete analysis
