Correlation and Intermarket Analysis

No market trades in isolation. Learn to read the invisible web connecting currencies, bonds, stocks, and commodities, and use it to your advantage.

One of the biggest mistakes new traders make is focusing on only one chart. They study the candlesticks, draw support and resistance, and wait for the perfect setup.

But here's the thing, markets don't move on their own. Different markets influence each other all the time. If you understand these relationships, you'll have a much better idea of what's happening behind the price movement.

Everything Is Connected

Think of the financial markets like a spider web. When one part of the web moves, the rest of it also feels the effect. A stronger US dollar can affect gold prices, higher oil prices can affect airline stocks, and rising bond yields can put pressure on technology stocks. None of these happen by accident, they're examples of intermarket relationships.

What Is Correlation?

Correlation measures how closely two markets move together. The value ranges from -1 to +1. A reading of +1 means both markets move together almost perfectly, -1 means they move in opposite directions, and 0 means there's little or no relationship between them. Understanding correlation helps you see whether two trades are really different, or simply the same idea in disguise.

Why Correlation Matters

Imagine you buy gold, silver, and a gold mining company. At first, it looks like three separate trades. In reality, they're all closely related, if gold drops sharply, there's a good chance all three positions will lose value together. That's why understanding correlation is also a form of risk management.

Common Market Relationships

US Dollar and Gold
Often Opposite
When the US dollar becomes stronger, gold often becomes weaker because it's more expensive for buyers using other currencies.
If you're trading gold, it's worth keeping an eye on the US Dollar Index (DXY).
Bond Yields and Growth Stocks
Often Opposite
Technology and growth stocks often struggle when bond yields rise, since higher yields make future company earnings less attractive.
This is one reason why the Nasdaq often reacts strongly to changes in bond yields.
Oil and Energy Stocks
Generally Positive
When oil prices rise, energy companies often generate higher profits, which can support their share prices.
The relationship isn't always immediate, but it's generally positive.
Oil and Airline Stocks
Often Opposite
Airlines spend a lot of money on fuel, so when oil prices increase, operating costs also increase.
Airline stocks often come under pressure when crude oil rises sharply.
Stocks and Cryptocurrency
Often Positive in Recent Years
When investors are confident, both markets tend to perform well. When fear enters the market, both can fall together.
This relationship isn't permanent, but it's something many traders watch.
US Dollar and Emerging Market Currencies
Often Opposite
A stronger US dollar often puts pressure on emerging market currencies, and can also affect stocks and bonds in those countries.
If you trade emerging markets, watching the dollar can provide useful context.
Correlations Don't Stay the Same Forever

A relationship that worked well last year may become much weaker this year. Major economic events, financial crises, central bank decisions, and changes in market sentiment can all affect how different markets behave. Don't assume a relationship will always stay the same, always check what's happening in the current market.

As a General Guide

Above +0.70: Strong positive relationship.
Below -0.70: Strong negative relationship.
Between -0.30 and +0.30: Weak or no meaningful relationship.
Anything in between: Moderate relationship that is worth monitoring. The stronger the number, the more likely the two markets are moving together.

How Traders Use Intermarket Analysis

Confirm a Trading Idea
Extra Confidence
  • If you're planning to buy gold and the US dollar is also weakening, that gives extra support to your idea
  • It doesn't guarantee success, but it adds confidence
  • If related markets send completely different signals, it might be worth waiting
Manage Your Risk
True Diversification
  • Owning different assets doesn't automatically mean you're diversified
  • If those assets all move together, the portfolio isn't really diversified
  • Understanding correlation helps you avoid taking too much risk without realizing it
Spot Early Warnings
Get Ahead of the Move
  • Some markets react before others, such as bond yields moving before stock prices respond
  • Watching related markets can give you an early warning that conditions are changing
  • It's not a guarantee, but it can provide valuable context

Common Mistakes

Assuming Correlations Never Change
Markets evolve. Always check current conditions instead of relying on old assumptions.
Thinking Different Assets Mean Different Risks
Owning several different assets doesn't automatically mean you're diversified. If they're highly correlated, you're still making one large bet.
Paying Too Much Attention to Weak Correlations
A correlation close to zero doesn't tell you very much. Don't make trading decisions based on very weak relationships.
Ignoring Other Markets
Looking at only one chart can cause you to miss the bigger picture. Sometimes the answer you're looking for is happening in a completely different market.

Final Thoughts

Every market is connected in one way or another. Learning these relationships won't tell you exactly where price is going next, but it gives you valuable context that many beginner traders overlook.

Intermarket analysis helps you understand why markets are moving, not just how they're moving.

When you combine good chart analysis with an understanding of market relationships, you'll make more informed trading decisions and manage your risk more effectively.

Key Takeaways

  • Markets are connected, and many assets influence one another
  • Correlation measures how closely two markets move together, from -1 to +1
  • Well-known relationships include the US dollar and gold, bond yields and growth stocks, and oil prices with airline or energy stocks
  • Correlations change over time, so always check current market conditions
  • Use intermarket analysis to confirm trade ideas and better manage overall portfolio risk
  • Different positions aren't always independent if they're highly correlated
