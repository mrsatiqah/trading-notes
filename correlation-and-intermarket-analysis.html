Correlation and Intermarket Analysis

No market trades in isolation. Learn to read the invisible web connecting currencies, bonds, stocks, and commodities, and use it to your advantage.

You've spent this series learning to read individual charts: candlesticks, support and resistance, trends, volume. But here's something most beginner traders miss entirely: no market moves alone.

When oil prices spike, airline stocks often fall. When the US dollar strengthens, gold often weakens. When bond yields rise, growth stocks often struggle. These aren't coincidences, they're intermarket relationships, and understanding them gives you context that pure chart-reading never can.

The Hidden Web

Think of global markets as a spider's web. Touch one strand, and the vibration travels to every other strand connected to it. Your job is to learn which strands are connected, and how strongly.

What Is Correlation, in Trading Terms?

Correlation measures how closely two assets move in relation to each other, expressed as a number between -1 and +1. A correlation of +1 means two assets move perfectly together. A correlation of -1 means they move in perfect opposite directions. A correlation near 0 means there's no meaningful relationship at all.

Why This Matters

If you're long three assets that are all highly correlated, you don't actually have three independent trades, you have one large concentrated bet wearing three different disguises.

Key Intermarket Relationships to Know

US Dollar vs Gold
Typically Negative
A stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for foreign buyers, often pushing gold prices down, and vice versa.
Watch DXY (dollar index) when trading gold positions.
Bond Yields vs Growth Stocks
Typically Negative
Rising yields increase the discount rate applied to future earnings, often pressuring high-growth, high-valuation stocks.
Tech-heavy indices are especially sensitive to yield moves.
Oil Prices vs Energy Stocks
Typically Positive
Rising crude prices generally boost the revenue and profit outlook for oil and gas producers.
Direct, intuitive relationship, but timing can lag.
Oil Prices vs Airline Stocks
Typically Negative
Fuel is one of the largest costs for airlines, so rising oil prices squeeze their margins directly.
Watch this pair around major oil supply news.
Risk Sentiment: Stocks vs Crypto
Often Positive in Recent Years
Both have increasingly traded as "risk-on" assets, rallying together during optimism and selling off together during fear.
This correlation isn't fixed, it shifts with market regime.
USD vs Emerging Market Currencies
Typically Negative
A strengthening dollar tends to pressure emerging market currencies and assets priced in those currencies.
Important context for anyone trading EM-linked assets.
Correlations Change

These relationships are tendencies, not laws. Correlations can weaken, strengthen, or even flip during unusual market regimes, crises, and structural shifts in the economy. Always verify current correlation rather than assuming it holds forever.

Practical Thresholds

Above +0.7 or below -0.7: Strong relationship, treat as connected positions.
Between -0.3 and +0.3: Weak or no meaningful relationship, can be treated as independent.
Everything in between: Moderate relationship, worth monitoring but not overreacting to.

Practical Ways to Use Intermarket Analysis

Confirm Your Bias
Cross-Market Confirmation
  • Check if related markets support your trade thesis
  • Dollar weakness supporting a gold long adds confidence
  • Conflicting signals across related markets are a warning sign
Manage True Portfolio Risk
Avoid Hidden Concentration
  • Reduce size across correlated positions, not just per trade
  • Recognize when "diversified" positions are actually one bet
  • Rebalance when correlation between your holdings increases
Anticipate Spillover Moves
Get Ahead of Reactions
  • Watch leading markets (like bond yields) for early signals
  • Related assets can move before their counterpart catches up
  • Use lag time as an early warning, not a certainty

Common Intermarket Analysis Mistakes

Assuming Static Correlations
Treating a relationship as permanent when correlations shift with market regime and macro conditions.
False Diversification
Believing a portfolio is diversified simply because it holds different asset names, without checking actual correlation.
Overreacting to Weak Correlations
Treating a 0.2 correlation as meaningful when it barely qualifies as a relationship at all.
Ignoring the Bigger Picture
Focusing only on a single chart while ignoring what related markets are signaling about broader risk sentiment.

Key Takeaways

  • No market trades in isolation, correlations connect currencies, bonds, stocks, and commodities
  • Correlation ranges from -1 (perfect opposite) to +1 (perfect together)
  • Key relationships include USD vs gold, yields vs growth stocks, and oil vs airlines
  • Correlations shift over time, always verify current relationships rather than assuming
  • Use intermarket analysis to confirm trade bias and manage true portfolio concentration
  • Correlated positions aren't independent bets, size your total exposure accordingly
